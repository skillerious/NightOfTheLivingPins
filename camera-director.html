Camera director overview

The camera should behave like a small arcade director: it frames the setup, follows the ball, sells the hit, lets the zombie pins react, then clearly returns the player to the foul line. It can be cinematic, but the ball, aim, pins, score state, and next input moment must always be understandable.

Build orderImplement in this order

This order keeps the camera useful while gameplay is still rough. It starts with the readable loop, then adds comedy and cinematic polish after the throw/reset flow works.

1Lock the player to the approachAdd FoulLineVolume, TV_ApproachArea, MinLaneY, and MaxLaneY. The player/ball can slide left and right, aim, charge, launch, and lightly steer, but never walk down-lane.
2Place camera target pointsAdd TP_Camera_PlayerView, TP_Camera_AimView, TP_Camera_BallFollow, TP_Camera_PinDeck, TP_Camera_Result, and TP_Camera_Reset before final art.
3Create the camera directorGive ANOLPCameraDirector target references, current state, blend settings, follow offsets, and event handlers. Do not spread camera decisions across the ball and pins.
4Prove the core throw loopRun PlayerView -> ChargeView -> LaunchView -> BallFollowView -> PinImpactView -> ResultView with temporary ball and pin placeholders.
5Add reset and click waitAfter scoring, clear knocked pins, let standing pins remain, show CLICK ANYWHERE TO PLAY, then blend back to the foul-line camera after click.
6Add surviving pin advanceWhen pins remain, shuffle them slightly toward the player with a capped distance. Tune by difficulty so it creates tension without becoming unfair.
7Add special momentsLayer in gutter tilt, strike camera, spare camera, open-frame taunts, and last-pin spare close-up only after the normal loop is stable.
8Test AI and multiplayer presentationUse the same camera states for AI and player turns. Add banners, delay beats, and clear turn ownership before launch.
Player staging and marker contractWhat the player sees before every throw

The default camera is behind the ball at the foul line, slightly above waist height, looking down the lane. It should feel spooky, but stable enough for accurate aiming.

Player view must show

  • The bowling ball in the foreground.
  • Lane arrows, centre path, gutters, and pin rack.
  • Aim line and power meter while aiming/charging.
  • Score, frame, throw number, and active player or AI name.
  • Zombie pins waiting at the end, with faces readable enough for comedy.
  • Hazards and power-ups without covering the clean ball path.

Subtle player-view motion

  • Gentle camera breathing while idle.
  • Small push-in when the mouse button is held.
  • Very small shake or rumble at maximum power.
  • Light flicker influence from the lane fixtures.
  • No heavy sway during aim. Accuracy beats atmosphere here.

Readable throw flowThe full loop the player should feel

This is the rhythm to test in Play In Editor before adding rare cinematic variants.

1. PromptLane is resolved. UI shows CLICK ANYWHERE TO PLAY. Input for aiming is still disabled.
2. ReturnClick starts a fast blend to TP_Camera_PlayerView. Ball resets to TP_BallSpawn.
3. PositionMouse X moves lane-space Y within MinLaneY and MaxLaneY.
4. ChargeHold mouse. Camera pushes in, power meter rises, ball glow increases, pins react in distance.
5. LaunchRelease. Short shake, aim line disappears, ball trail appears, camera blends to follow.
6. FollowCamera tracks behind and above, looking ahead. If the ball drifts, gutter feedback is clear.
7. ImpactNear the deck, cut or push to show the collision, reaction, and pin scatter.
8. SettleWait for physics to settle. Count pins after they stop, not at first contact.
9. AdvanceStanding zombie pins may twitch and shuffle toward the player, capped by difficulty.
10. RepeatShow result and next prompt. The player clicks to begin the next throw or turn.
Scenario playbooksHow each special moment should work

Each playbook has a trigger, camera action, feedback layer, and exit rule. This keeps comedy moments from dragging or confusing the player.

Normal hit

Trigger
Ball reaches PinDeckCameraTriggerDistance or hits a pin.
Camera
Continue ball follow into deck for small hits; cut to pin deck angle for strong hits.
Feedback
Impact sound, short shake, pins scream or tumble, score popup after settle.
Exit
When ball reaches pit/stops and pins settle, enter ResultView.

Gutter

Trigger
Ball overlaps TV_Gutter_L or TV_Gutter_R.
Camera
Tilt toward the gutter and follow long enough to prove what happened.
Feedback
GUTTER!, gutter roll audio, reduced steering, zombie laugh or machine chuckle.
Exit
Resolve as soon as the ball reaches pit or stops. No long punishment shot.

Last-pin spare drama

Trigger
Throw two, one pin standing, ball active, predicted path likely hits final pin.
Camera
Dynamic close-up from final pin transform or Socket_FaceCameraTarget.
Feedback
Brief slow motion, eyes widen, default voice line Uh oh..., impact shake.
Exit
Maximum 1-2 seconds or impact. Then cut to SpareView if the pin falls.

Strike

Trigger
All pins down on first throw after settle.
Camera
Low impact cam, overhead chaos cam, ball POV, pin face cam, or machine reaction cam.
Feedback
STRIKE!, big sound, sparks, machine anger, brief slow motion only for strong hits.
Exit
Return to result/reset after the celebration beat, not after every pin animation finishes.

Open frame

Trigger
Pins remain after the allowed throws.
Camera
Hold on the remaining pins and show them as alive, smug, and dangerous.
Feedback
Laugh, point, wobble, tiny victory dance, score update, OPEN FRAME.
Exit
Advance surviving pins if allowed, then show click prompt.

AI or local turn

Trigger
Turn owner changes.
Camera
Show a banner, return to player view or AI setup view, then follow the AI shot normally.
Feedback
PLAYER 2 - THROW 2 or ROTTEN RANDY IS LINING UP A SHOT....
Exit
Same result/reset/click flow, so the match rhythm stays consistent.

Surviving pin advance rule

Standing pins are not just leftovers. They become the zombie pressure system.

  • Clear or disable knocked pins after scoring.
  • Keep standing pins active and facing the player.
  • Shuffle them slightly toward the foul line.
  • Add random offset so movement feels organic.
  • Clamp by MaxSurvivingPinAdvanceDistance.
  • Easy: little or none. Normal: weak throws. Hard: every non-clear. Nightmare: more aggressive plus taunts.

Review checklistUse this before calling the camera pass done
Foul-line ruleThe player can move and aim in the approach area but cannot cross the foul line during active gameplay.
Readable player viewFrom the default view, ball, lane, aim line, power, pins, hazards, score, and turn ownership are visible.
Readable ball followThe camera is not so close that lane hazards, gutters, or pin deck are lost.
Short cinematic beatsGutter, strike, spare, and last-pin moments are punchy and do not block understanding.
Reset clarityThe result holds long enough to read score and standing pins before pin clear/advance begins.
Zombie advance fairnessSurviving pins move enough to create tension but never so far that the next throw feels unfair.
Click-to-play rhythmAfter reset, input waits for click. After click, camera return completes before aiming input is active.
AI/multiplayer clarityThe active player or AI is announced before the throw, and the camera flow does not change rules between competitors.
Camera personality target: the camera is part of the game's personality. The player should remember the ball racing down the lane, the frame shaking when it hits slime, the final zombie pin whispering Uh oh..., surviving pins creeping closer after a weak throw, and the cursed machine snapping the view back to the foul line for one more shot.
